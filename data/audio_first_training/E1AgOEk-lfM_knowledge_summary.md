### 2. Inducement Shift Rule 1: No Sweep, New High/Low
*Teaching Duration: ~55s | Word Count: ~180*

If the market did NOT sweep the current inducement and created a new high/low, inducement shifts to the new valid pullback (first valid pullback on the left side of the new high/low). The old inducement is abandoned and the new pullback becomes the active inducement target. Demonstrated on a TradingView chart with an uptrend showing price bypassing the current inducement to make a new high.

---

### 3. Inducement Shift Rule 2: Sweep Without Close Below
*Teaching Duration: ~80s | Word Count: ~250*

If the market DID sweep inducement but the candle did NOT close below it (only wicked through), and created a new high/low, the sweep swing itself becomes inducement. Critical nuance: after this scenario, there can be TWO active inducement levels simultaneously -- the sweep swing low and the first valid pullback on the left side of the new high. Market may take out just one or both inducement levels before continuing. Factors like price imbalance and order block locations determine which gets hunted.

### 4. Inducement Shift Rule 3: Impulse Swing Rule (Most Critical)
*Teaching Duration: ~120s | Word Count: ~380*

If market swept inducement AND candle closed below it, but then created a new high/low with an IMPULSE SWING (no pullback), the low of that swing acts as inducement, NOT as a key structural level. This is explicitly called "the most critical and important rule" because it is where most retail and conventional SMC traders get trapped. Retail traders see the impulse-created HL as a key support level. When price breaks it, they sell (expecting CHoCH). True SMC traders recognize it as inducement and buy with the original trend. Chart example shows HH and HL labels, with annotations showing retail traders selling at what they think is CHoCH while smart money buys.

### 5. Smart Money Traps (via Inducement)
*Teaching Duration: ~90s | Word Count: ~280*

Traps occur when institutional players engineer price movements that lure retail traders into the wrong side. The primary trap in this video: retail traders mistake an impulse-created higher low for a key level, trade the apparent CHoCH, and get trapped when price reverses back with the original trend. Two groups get caught: retail traders (basic TA) and conventional SMC traders (who understand CHoCH but not the impulse swing rule). Avoidance: always check if a swing was created by an impulse move; if so, it is inducement, not a key level.

### 6. Inducement and Order Blocks Relationship
*Teaching Duration: ~60s | Word Count: ~200*

Inducement acts as a trap BEFORE an order block (supply/demand zone). Having inducement in front of an order block increases setup probability. The market sweeps inducement to collect liquidity, then taps into the order block for the actual entry. Trade setup: Entry at order block (after inducement swept), TP at previous high, SL below previous short-term low. When price imbalance exists between inducement and the order block, market sweeps multiple inducement levels to balance the imbalance before continuing.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Inducement shifts when market structure evolves** -- it is not a static level. Traders must continuously update their inducement markup.
2. **Three rules govern how inducement shifts**: no sweep (shifts to new pullback), sweep without close (sweep swing becomes inducement), and impulse swing (swing low is inducement, not a key level).
3. **Rule 3 is the most critical** because it creates the trap that catches both retail and conventional SMC traders.
4. **Impulse swings create inducement, not key levels** -- this is the single most important distinction for avoiding traps.
5. **Inducement before an order block increases setup probability** -- the more liquidity swept before reaching the OB, the more likely it holds.
6. **Everything in SMC depends on correct inducement identification** -- the dependency loop shows that wrong inducement leads to wrong swing points, wrong structure, wrong BOS, wrong CHoCH, and ultimately failed trades.
