## 1. High Probability Inducement

*Teaching time: 5.9 min | Words: 850 | Units: 18 | Frames: 6*

### Definition
High probability inducement refers to the inducement level within a price swing that carries the greatest likelihood of producing a significant price reaction when taken out by the market. In ICT methodology, inducement is a valid pullback within a swing that creates a level where retail stop-losses and pending orders accumulate. When a swing contains multiple inducement levels, the high probability inducement is the one associated with the deepest pullback (the major inducement).

### Identification Rules
1. **Classify each pullback in the swing**: Compare the depth of each pullback relative to others in the same swing.
2. **Deeper pullback = Major Inducement = High Probability**: The pullback that retraces the most deeply is classified as major inducement (high probability).
3. **Shorter pullbacks = Minor Inducement = Low Probability**: Pullbacks that retrace only a small portion of the swing are minor inducement levels.
4. **Single inducement rule**: If only one inducement exists, it is automatically the high probability inducement.
5. **Same-size rule**: If all pullbacks are approximately the same depth, the FIRST inducement is considered high probability.
6. **Visual method**: Compare the vertical depth of pullbacks on the chart; the deepest is the major inducement.

### Trading Application
- Plan setups primarily around the high probability (major) inducement level.
- The magnitude of the resulting move depends on the inducement size: minor inducement produces minor moves, major inducement produces major moves.
- Works on any timeframe (demonstrated on H1 with EURUSD and GBPUSD).
- Use high probability inducement as the primary filter for trade setups rather than trading every inducement.

## 2. Minor vs Major Inducement

*Teaching time: 1.8 min | Words: 280 | Units: 6 | Frames: 3*

### Definition
Inducement levels within a price swing are classified into two categories based on pullback depth:
- **Minor Inducement**: Created by a shorter (shallower) pullback within the swing.
- **Major Inducement**: Created by a deeper pullback within the swing.

### Identification
- **Minor Inducement**: Shallow pullback compared to other pullbacks in the same swing. Multiple minor inducements can exist in one swing.
- **Major Inducement**: The deepest pullback in the swing. Typically only one major inducement per swing.
- **Classification is relative**: Compare pullback depths within the SAME swing. A pullback is not inherently minor or major; it depends on its size relative to others.
- **Three schematic examples demonstrated**: (a) Bullish swing with two minor + one major; (b) Bearish swing with one minor + one major; (c) Bullish swing with two minor + one major.

### Trading Application
- Prioritize major inducement levels for trade planning.
- Minor inducements produce minor reactions; major inducements produce major moves.
- Helps avoid false entries on minor inducements that lead to small reactions.

## 3. Same Size Inducement Rule

*Teaching time: 1.1 min | Words: 160 | Units: 4 | Frames: 2*

### Definition
When a price swing contains multiple inducement levels and all pullbacks are approximately the same depth, the FIRST inducement level in the swing is considered the high probability inducement. This rule resolves ambiguity when the standard major/minor classification cannot be applied.

### Identification
1. Compare all pullback depths -- if roughly equal, the major/minor classification does not apply.
2. No dominant deeper pullback exists among the inducements.
3. Also applies to single inducement swings (automatically high probability).
4. Demonstrated with a EURUSD H1 chart showing same-size inducements.

### Trading Application
- Focus setups on the first inducement level when all are the same size.
- After the first inducement is taken, expect the most significant price move.
- Prevents confusion when the depth-comparison method yields no clear winner.

## 4. Engineered Liquidity

*Teaching time: 4.8 min | Words: 750 | Units: 16 | Frames: 6*

### Definition
Engineered liquidity (ENG LIQ) is an area on the chart where smart money deliberately creates fake support and resistance levels to trap retail traders and accumulate their orders as liquidity. Smart money manipulates price to form patterns that retail traders recognize as valid (support, resistance, channels, flags), causing them to place orders at these levels. Once sufficient liquidity is accumulated, smart money grabs it with a fast, efficient move and drives price in the opposite direction.

### Identification
1. **Fake support levels**: Price tests a level multiple times, tricking retail traders into believing it is strong support.
2. **Fake resistance levels**: Price tests a level from below, trapping retail sellers.
3. **Channel/flag patterns**: Consolidation in channel or flag formations where retail pattern traders place orders at trendline boundaries.
4. **Equal highs/equal lows**: Double-top or double-bottom formations at the same price level represent engineered liquidity.
5. **Breakout traps**: After consolidation, price breaks out and retail traders add orders in the breakout direction, creating additional engineered liquidity.
6. **Schematic diagram**: Shows labeled zones at fake support levels, trendline intersections, and equal highs/lows.
7. **Real chart annotations**: Boxes and labels showing where retail S/R levels trap traders before price grabs liquidity.

### Trading Application
- Do NOT place orders at obvious support/resistance, trendline touches, or pattern breakouts -- these are engineered liquidity traps.
- Price reacts to engineered liquidity with fast, efficient moves before reversing.
- After price grabs liquidity from one side, expect it to target the opposite side's pool.
- Critical for identifying fake change of character (CHOCH) levels (covered in the next lecture).
- Trade with institutional flow rather than getting caught in retail traps.

## 5. Liquidity Pool Flow

*Teaching time: 1.3 min | Words: 200 | Units: 4 | Frames: 3*

### Definition
Price moves from one pool of liquidity to another. Once price grabs liquidity from one side, it targets the opposite side's liquidity pool. This explains the fundamental mechanism by which smart money drives price: engineering liquidity on one side, grabbing it, then targeting the opposite pool.

### Identification
1. **Identify pools on both sides**: Buy-side liquidity above highs (equal highs, swing highs, resistance) and sell-side liquidity below lows (equal lows, swing lows, support).
2. **Track the grab sequence**: When price sweeps one pool, the next target is the opposing pool.
3. **Real market example**: Price repeatedly alternates between grabbing sell-side and buy-side engineered liquidity.

### Trading Application
- After liquidity is grabbed from one side, anticipate a move toward the opposite pool.
- If sell-side liquidity was just taken, look for long setups targeting buy-side liquidity.
- Combine with engineered liquidity identification to map out price targets.
- The video demonstrates this by having the viewer predict price direction after each grab.

## 6. Retail Trader Traps

*Teaching time: 2.0 min | Words: 250 | Units: 6 | Frames: 4*

### Definition
Retail trader traps are market scenarios deliberately engineered by smart money to lure retail traders into losing positions. These traps exploit common retail methodologies -- support/resistance trading, trendline trading, and chart pattern trading -- by creating price structures that appear to validate these methods, only to reverse once sufficient retail orders are accumulated.

### Identification
1. **Support/Resistance traps**: Price tests a level two or more times, convincing retail traders it is reliable. Orders accumulate, then smart money drives through.
2. **Trendline/Channel traps**: Price forms visible channels or flags. Retail trendline traders execute at boundaries. Example: rising bearish flag where retail expected a downward move.
3. **Breakout traps**: After consolidation, price breaks out, retail traders add orders, then price reverses.
4. **Dual perspective analysis**: The video examines the SAME chart from both a support/resistance trader's viewpoint and a trendline trader's viewpoint, showing both groups get trapped.

### Trading Application
- Avoid pure retail methodology (S/R bounces, trendline touches, pattern breakouts) without understanding smart money.
- Obvious technical levels are targets for liquidity grabs, not reliable entry points.
- When clear retail patterns form, consider that smart money may be engineering liquidity there.
- Be a smart money trader, not a retail trader.

## 7. Inducement Magnitude Principle

*Teaching time: 0.9 min | Words: 150 | Units: 3 | Frames: 2*

### Definition
The size of the price move after an inducement is taken out is proportional to the size (depth) of the inducement itself. Minor inducement leads to a minor price move; major inducement leads to a major price move.

### Identification
1. **Assess inducement depth**: Measure or visually estimate pullback depth.
2. **Minor inducement taken = minor move**: Resulting move is smaller and potentially temporary.
3. **Major inducement taken = major move**: Resulting move is larger and more sustained.
4. **EURUSD H1 evidence**: Minor inducement produced a small reaction upward; major inducement produced a much larger move.

### Trading Application
- Do not expect a full swing move from a minor inducement grab.
- Wait for the major inducement to be taken before expecting the primary move.
- Helps with entry timing (wait for high probability level) and target setting (larger targets after major inducement grabs).
- Avoid premature entries on minor inducement reactions.
